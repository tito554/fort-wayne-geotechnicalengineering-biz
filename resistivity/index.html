The glacial geology of Allen County creates a complex layered subsurface where clay-rich tills pinch out against coarse outwash sands. In Fort Wayne, seasonal saturation of these units shifts background resistivity values by 30 to 50 percent between spring and fall, making baseline characterization critical before any excavation or well installation. Our field crew deploys a 4-pin Wenner array and a multi-electrode LUND system for MASW and seismic velocity profiling when combined geophysical models are required. Vertical Electrical Sounding complements traditional SPT drilling by resolving the contact between the Trafalgar Formation aquitard and the underlying sand-and-gravel aquifer without mobilizing a drill rig. For brownfield sites near the St. Marys River, resistivity imaging helps pinpoint leachate plumes before in-situ permeability testing is scheduled, saving clients thousands in unnecessary borehole costs.

A 4-electrode Wenner spread on Fort Wayne glacial till can distinguish a 2-meter sand lens at 12 meters depth when the water table is above the target.

Our approach and scope

The resistivity meter we mobilize around Fort Wayne is an ABEM Terrameter system with 64 programmable electrodes, capable of switching between Schlumberger and dipole-dipole configurations in a single survey line. Current injection ranges from 0.2 to 500 mA depending on contact resistance, which in local silty clay can exceed 2 kOhm during dry spells. To overcome this, the crew saturates electrode holes with a bentonite-saline slurry that drops contact resistance below 500 Ohm. Data inversion runs on Res2DInv with a least-squares smoothness constraint, and we cross-check the inverted section against IDNR water well logs when profiling near the Huntertown till plain. The method pairs naturally with grain-size distribution analysis when the resistivity boundary between coarse and fine units needs a direct textural calibration from hollow-stem auger samples. Where the Maumee River floodplain thickens near downtown, we extend survey lines to 400 meters to ensure the current penetrates below the 20-meter depth of investigation required for bridge pier design.

Local geotechnical context

Fort Wayne sits above a buried bedrock valley where glacial drift thickness exceeds 100 meters in places, and the upper 15 meters alternate between low-resistivity lake clays and high-resistivity sand stringers. Misinterpreting a 25 Ohm-m clay as a dry sand in a VES sounding can lead to placing a production well screen in a low-yield unit, forcing costly re-drilling. The risk is highest along the Maumee River corridor, where anthropogenic fill with brick fragments and cinders creates a surface layer with apparent resistivity above 200 Ohm-m that masks the true stratigraphy. Our team runs forward models with a 3-layer earth assumption before finalizing the survey geometry, adjusting electrode spacing so the current electrode separation reaches at least four times the target depth. For sites with suspected liquefaction-prone silts in the abandoned meander belts, we combine electrical resistivity with CPT tip resistance logs to verify the absence of thin saturated lenses that a single-geometry VES might miss.

Quick answers

How deep can a VES survey reach in the Fort Wayne glacial deposits?

Maximum depth of investigation depends on the current electrode separation AB and the subsurface resistivity contrast. With a maximum AB/2 of 200 meters on an open site, the current penetrates to approximately 60 to 80 meters in the silty-clay matrix typical of the Trafalgar Formation. At sites near the Huntertown moraine, where near-surface sand increases bulk resistivity above 80 Ohm-m, the practical depth limit is closer to 50 meters with the same geometry. We design the electrode expansion schedule after reviewing nearby IDNR well logs to ensure the target aquifer falls within the resolved depth interval.

What terrain conditions limit resistivity surveys near the Fort Wayne rivers?

The main limitations in the river corridors are high contact resistance in dry surface soils, buried metallic utilities along Clinton Street and Spy Run Avenue corridors, and cultural noise from 60 Hz power lines. Our crew uses a spectrum analyzer to select a frequency band with minimal background noise, typically 2.5 to 5 Hz. In paved areas near downtown, we drill small-diameter pilot holes through asphalt to reach native soil for electrode coupling, restoring the surface afterward with cold patch.

Can resistivity imaging detect contamination from the old industrial sites along the St. Marys?

Yes, electrical resistivity is sensitive to changes in pore-fluid conductivity caused by leachate, salt-impacted groundwater, or hydrocarbon degradation byproducts. At former manufacturing sites near the St. Marys River, we have mapped conductive plumes with apparent resistivity below 15 Ohm-m embedded in a background of 40 to 60 Ohm-m clay. Time-lapse surveys, repeating the same line at seasonal high and low water table, help distinguish mobile contamination from natural variations in clay mineralogy. A direct-push or hollow-stem auger sampling program always follows the geophysics to confirm the chemical signature of any anomaly.
